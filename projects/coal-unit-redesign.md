  <section class="section" aria-labelledby="problem-h">
    <p class="sec-label"><span class="sec-num">07</span> Problem Statement &amp; Commission</p>
    <h2 id="problem-h">A Grinding Unit Running Below Its Mechanical Ceiling — With the Kiln Waiting</h2>

    <p>
      In cement manufacturing, the coal grinding unit occupies a critical position in the energy chain: it supplies the pulverised fuel that fires the rotary kiln, and its output rate directly limits the kiln's coal feed rate. When the grinding unit is the bottleneck — running below its mechanical throughput ceiling while the kiln has headroom to consume more — every tonne of coal-per-hour lost at the mill translates directly into unrealised clinker production at the other end of the pyro-processing line.
    </p>
    <p>
      The coal grinding unit at Attock Cement Limited was running at 21 TPH. The unit's mechanical design had capacity for more — but extracting it without understanding why throughput was constrained risked two specific failure modes: mill choking, where excess feed rate causes material to back up in the grinding circuit faster than the separator and air system can clear it; and moisture exceedance, where increased throughput overloads the heat exchange capacity of the drying system, pushing coal moisture above the 1% threshold at which grindability deteriorates and downstream combustion quality is compromised.
    </p>
    <p>
      The project objective was not simply to run the mill faster. It was to understand exactly which parameters were holding throughput at 21 TPH, identify the adjustments that could raise the ceiling without triggering either failure mode, and validate the result with energy audits rigorous enough to demonstrate that the gain was real — not a temporary operating point that would degrade SEC or revert under normal operating variation.
    </p>

    <div class="note-box green">
      <strong>Objective:</strong> Redesign the operating parameters of the coal grinding unit to sustainably increase throughput from 21 TPH to the highest achievable rate consistent with stable SEC, coal moisture below 1%, and no mill choking — then validate the outcome with before-and-after energy audits and calculate the annualised financial return.
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="section" aria-labelledby="method-h">
    <p class="sec-label"><span class="sec-num">08</span> Methodology &amp; Execution</p>
    <h2 id="method-h">A Four-Lever Redesign — Feed, Separation, Moisture, and Air, in Sequence</h2>

    <p>
      The redesign was structured around four interdependent operating parameters, each adjusted in sequence rather than simultaneously — because in a closed-circuit grinding system, simultaneous multi-variable changes make it impossible to attribute throughput response to any single intervention. Each parameter was adjusted, the system was allowed to stabilise, and SEC was recalculated before the next lever was moved.
    </p>

    <div class="method-steps">

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">01</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Energy Audit &amp; Mass Balance — Before-State Baseline</h3>
          <p>Before any parameter was adjusted, a full before-state energy audit was conducted to establish the baseline SEC and confirm the actual operating throughput under normal conditions. The mass balance across the grinding circuit was closed — inlet coal feed rate, product discharge rate, and circulating load were all measured independently and reconciled to ensure the baseline figure was accurate, not a control system reading that might differ from the physical flow. This step was not administrative procedure; a mass balance that does not close reveals a measurement error in the data being used to calculate SEC, and an SEC calculated from erroneous flow data cannot be used to validate a redesign outcome.</p>
          <div class="step-tags">
            <span class="step-tag">Before-State Audit</span>
            <span class="step-tag">Mass Balance Closure</span>
            <span class="step-tag blue">SEC Baseline Established</span>
            <span class="step-tag">Circulating Load Measured</span>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">02</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Feed Rate &amp; Separator Speed Optimisation — Eliminating the Choking Constraint</h3>
          <p>The primary constraint on throughput was identified as separator speed miscalibration relative to the actual feed rate: the separator was rejecting too high a proportion of material back into the grinding circuit as circulating load, creating a self-limiting feedback loop where increased feed caused circuit overloading rather than increased product output. Separator speed was adjusted to reduce the circulating load fraction, and the feed rate was increased incrementally — stopping at each step to confirm that the product fineness remained within specification and that the mill differential pressure (the key choking indicator) remained stable. This iterative approach, rather than a step-change to the target feed rate, was the method that allowed the new operating point to be found without triggering the choking failure mode.</p>

            <span class="step-tag">Feed Rate Incremented</span>
            <span class="step-tag blue">ΔP Monitored — No Choking</span>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">03</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Thermal Stability Monitoring — Holding Moisture Below 1% at Increased Throughput</h3>
          <p>Increasing the feed rate through the mill also increases the moisture load presented to the drying system — because higher-throughput coal carries more total moisture even at the same inlet moisture content. The heat exchange balance was monitored continuously as throughput increased to verify that the drying system retained sufficient capacity to maintain outlet coal moisture below 1%. Inlet gas temperature, mill outlet temperature, and product moisture were logged at each feed rate step. Where the thermal margin tightened, hot gas flow was adjusted to maintain the drying rate without exceeding the temperature limits that would compromise the coal's combustion properties or create fire risk in the mill. Thermal stability was the binding constraint that set the final sustainable throughput ceiling at 25 TPH.</p>

        <div class="step-num">04</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Primary Air Fan Tuning &amp; After-State Validation — Confirming SEC and Financial Return</h3>
          <p>At the new 25 TPH operating point, the primary air fan settings were fine-tuned to match the higher coal volume being transported through the pneumatic conveying system — ensuring that the increased product rate was moved through the circuit without increasing the specific conveying energy per tonne. The after-state energy audit was then conducted under the same measurement protocol as the before-state baseline: mass balance closed, motor power metered, and SEC calculated from verified flow and power data rather than control system estimates. The before-and-after SEC figures matched within measurement uncertainty, confirming that the throughput gain was genuine efficiency improvement. The annualised financial return of 7M PKR was calculated from the sustained throughput increment applied against the plant's operating cost and production value data.</p>

    <p class="table-caption" style="margin-top:1.8rem;">Python SEC Audit Engine — Before-vs-After Validation Function</p>
    <div class="code-block">
      <pre><span class="c-cm"># ── SEC Audit & Throughput Validation Engine ─────────────────</span>
<span class="c-cm"># Calculates Specific Energy Consumption from metered power and</span>
<span class="c-cm"># mass-balanced flow data, then compares before/after states.</span>

<span class="c-kw">def</span> <span class="c-fn">calculate_sec</span>(
    power_kw: <span class="c-fn">float</span>,
    throughput_tph: <span class="c-fn">float</span>
) -&gt; <span class="c-fn">float</span>:
    <span class="c-str">"""
    Returns Specific Energy Consumption in kWh/tonne.
    Both inputs must be from closed mass balance and
    metered power — not control system estimates.
    """</span>
    <span class="c-kw">if</span> throughput_tph <span class="c-op">&lt;=</span> <span class="c-num">0</span>:
        <span class="c-kw">raise</span> <span class="c-fn">ValueError</span>(<span class="c-str">"Throughput must be positive — check mass balance."</span>)
    <span class="c-kw">return</span> power_kw <span class="c-op">/</span> throughput_tph   <span class="c-cm"># kWh/tonne</span>


<span class="c-kw">def</span> <span class="c-fn">validate_redesign</span>(
    before: <span class="c-fn">dict</span>,
    after:  <span class="c-fn">dict</span>,
    sec_tolerance: <span class="c-fn">float</span> <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">0.03</span>     <span class="c-cm"># ±3% — measurement uncertainty band</span>
) -&gt; <span class="c-fn">dict</span>:
    <span class="c-str">"""
    Compares before/after audit snapshots.
    Returns throughput gain, SEC delta, and validation verdict.
    sec_tolerance: fractional SEC change considered 'stable'.
    """</span>
    sec_before = <span class="c-fn">calculate_sec</span>(before[<span class="c-str">"power_kw"</span>], before[<span class="c-str">"throughput_tph"</span>])
    sec_after  = <span class="c-fn">calculate_sec</span>(after[<span class="c-str">"power_kw"</span>],  after[<span class="c-str">"throughput_tph"</span>])

    throughput_gain_pct = (after[<span class="c-str">"throughput_tph"</span>] <span class="c-op">-</span> before[<span class="c-str">"throughput_tph"</span>]) \
                          <span class="c-op">/</span> before[<span class="c-str">"throughput_tph"</span>] <span class="c-op">*</span> <span class="c-num">100</span>
    sec_delta_pct       = (sec_after <span class="c-op">-</span> sec_before) <span class="c-op">/</span> sec_before <span class="c-op">*</span> <span class="c-num">100</span>
    sec_stable          = <span class="c-fn">abs</span>(sec_delta_pct) <span class="c-op">&lt;=</span> (sec_tolerance <span class="c-op">*</span> <span class="c-num">100</span>)

    <span class="c-kw">return</span> {
        <span class="c-str">"throughput_gain_pct"</span> : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(throughput_gain_pct, <span class="c-num">1</span>),
        <span class="c-str">"sec_before_kwh_t"</span>   : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(sec_before, <span class="c-num">3</span>),
        <span class="c-str">"sec_after_kwh_t"</span>    : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(sec_after,  <span class="c-num">3</span>),

  <section class="section" aria-labelledby="findings-h">
    <p class="sec-label"><span class="sec-num">11</span> Key Findings &amp; Learning Outcomes</p>
    <h2 id="findings-h">What Redesigning a Grinding Circuit from the Inside Actually Teaches You</h2>
    <div class="findings-grid">

      <div class="finding-card">
        <span class="find-pill find">Finding</span>
        <h3>Separator Miscalibration Was the Hidden Ceiling — Not Motor Power</h3>
        <p>The instinct when a grinding unit is underperforming is to look at motor load or feed rate as the primary constraint. In this case, the actual ceiling was separator speed: the classifier was returning too high a circulating load fraction to the grinding chamber, creating internal circuit overloading that capped net product output regardless of how much feed was introduced. Identifying this required looking at the full mass balance across the circuit — not just the feed and product ends — which is why the mass balance closure step in the methodology was non-negotiable.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card blue-accent">
        <span class="find-pill learn">Learning</span>
        <h3>Throughput and SEC Must Be Optimised Together, or the Result Is Not an Improvement</h3>
        <p>Increasing throughput without tracking SEC is not process optimisation — it is simply running a machine harder. The distinction matters because a throughput gain accompanied by proportionally higher energy draw produces no financial benefit once the increased electricity cost is accounted for. The SEC audit was not an add-on to this project; it was the core validation instrument. Without it, the 19.1% throughput figure would have been a production statistic rather than a confirmed efficiency gain with a defensible financial return.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card amber-accent">
        <span class="find-pill design">Design</span>
        <h3>Moisture Is the Thermal Boundary Condition That Sets the Throughput Ceiling</h3>
        <p>In any coal grinding system with integrated drying, the thermal capacity of the heat exchange system — not the mechanical grinding capacity — sets the upper throughput limit. As feed rate increases, total moisture load increases proportionally. The point at which the drying system can no longer maintain outlet moisture below 1% is the point at which further feed rate increases degrade grindability and downstream combustion quality. In this project, 25 TPH was the sustainable ceiling precisely because 25 TPH was the rate at which the drying system remained within its thermal operating envelope.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card">
        <span class="find-pill find">Finding</span>
        <h3>Incremental Parameter Changes Reveal System Response That Step Changes Cannot</h3>
        <p>The decision to increase feed rate incrementally — rather than stepping directly to the target throughput — was methodologically important. Each incremental step produced a measurable system response: mill differential pressure, product moisture, and separator load all adjusted to the new operating point before the next increment was applied. This step-by-step approach was the reason that the sustainable throughput ceiling could be identified precisely at 25 TPH, rather than discovered retroactively as a choked or moisture-failed mill at some higher uncontrolled feed rate.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card blue-accent">
        <span class="find-pill learn">Learning</span>
        <h3>A Financial Return Must Be Calculated from Verified Operating Data to Be Defensible</h3>
        <p>The 7M PKR annual savings figure was calculated from audited, mass-balance-confirmed throughput data and metered power readings — not from control system displays or design estimates. This distinction matters because financial returns calculated from unverified data cannot withstand scrutiny when presented to plant management or used in capital allocation decisions. The audit protocol used in this project was designed from the outset so that every number in the financial calculation had a verified physical measurement behind it.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card">
        <span class="find-pill find">Finding</span>
        <h3>Air-to-Fuel Ratio Tuning Is the Final Trim That Holds the Gain Sustainably</h3>
        <p>The primary air fan adjustment was the last lever applied, after throughput, separation, and thermal stability had been addressed. It would have been ineffective — or counterproductive — applied earlier: the correct air-to-fuel ratio for pneumatic transport of pulverised coal depends on the product flow rate, which was still changing during the earlier optimisation steps. Applying the air fan tuning last, once the new operating point was confirmed and stable, meant it was calibrated against the actual sustained throughput rather than an intermediate value — which is why the gain held over time rather than drifting back.</p>
      </div>

    </div>
