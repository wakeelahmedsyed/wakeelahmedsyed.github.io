  <section class="section" aria-labelledby="problem-h">
    <p class="sec-label"><span class="sec-num">07</span> Problem Statement &amp; Commission</p>
    <h2 id="problem-h">A 250M PKR Investment With No Guarantee the Underlying Assumptions Were Right</h2>

    <p>
      Capital system upgrades in industrial cement manufacturing are high-stakes engineering commitments: the equipment is bespoke, lead times are long, and the cost of a failure discovered post-installation — whether through a ducting geometry that cannot support design airflow, a pneumatic actuator that defaults to the wrong position on power loss, or a PLC sequence that triggers a mechanical surge on start-up — is measured not just in repair cost but in unplanned downtime across the production chain.
    </p>
    <p>
      When a 250M PKR system upgrade reached the commissioning stage at Attock Cement Limited, the critical questions had not yet received formal engineering answers: Could the existing fan curve actually support the new ducting geometry at design airflow and static pressure? Were all 50+ I/O points wired, labelled, and responding correctly? Were the pneumatic actuators configured to fail in the safe position? And had the PLC interlocking logic been written with the correct start-up sequence — one that would ramp equipment in the right order before exposing downstream components to full system pressure?
    </p>
    <p>
      These are not questions that can be answered by reviewing a supplier datasheet or trusting a design drawing. They require independent verification against real plant conditions — which is what the commissioning phase was designed to do, and what this project executed end-to-end.
    </p>

    <div class="note-box green">
      <strong>Commission:</strong> Independently validate the full technical and control system readiness of a 250M PKR process unit upgrade — confirming fan-curve adequacy through first-principles ΔP calculation, verifying all I/O points and interlock logic through systematic cold commissioning, and producing the definitive operational SOP and operator training programme for system handover.
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="section" aria-labelledby="method-h">
    <p class="sec-label"><span class="sec-num">08</span> Methodology &amp; Execution</p>
    <h2 id="method-h">A Four-Phase Workflow — From Calculation to Cold Test to Handover</h2>

    <p>
      The commissioning framework was structured so that each phase had to be signed off before the next could begin — preventing the common failure mode of commissioning projects where field observations are rushed to meet a go-live date without resolving open technical questions. Every verification step was documented, traceable, and independent of the design team's own assumptions.
    </p>

    <div class="method-steps">

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">01</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Independent Technical Validation — First-Principles ΔP Calculation</h3>
          <p>Before any physical commissioning work began, the central design assumption was interrogated: could the existing fan curve support the new ducting geometry at the required airflow and static pressure? Rather than accepting the design-team estimate, an independent pressure drop calculation was performed from first principles — accounting for the actual duct lengths, bends, fittings, and entry/exit conditions in the upgraded configuration. The resulting ΔP verified that the fan could deliver 12,050 m³/h against 4.15 kPa static pressure, confirming design adequacy and — critically — identifying that the motor was running 3 percentage points below its rated load, providing useful headroom margin for fouling conditions.</p>
          <div class="step-tags">
            <span class="step-tag">First-Principles ΔP</span>
            <span class="step-tag">Fan Curve Analysis</span>
            <span class="step-tag blue">Independent of Design Team</span>
            <span class="step-tag">Headroom Margin Identified</span>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">02</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Cold Commissioning — 50+ I/O Point Verification &amp; Interlock Testing</h3>
          <p>Cold commissioning — system testing with control power on but no live process flow — was used to verify every input and output point in the upgraded system before any equipment was energised at operating conditions. Each of the 50+ I/O points was individually actuated, labelled, and confirmed against the control system loop diagram: analogue signals were ranged and calibrated, digital inputs were verified to register the correct logic state, and every pneumatic actuator was tested for its fail-safe default position under a simulated loss of instrument air. This step-by-step approach — rather than a bulk loop check — was the method that made the PLC sequencing fault detectable.</p>
          <div class="step-tags">
            <span class="step-tag amber">50+ I/O Points</span>
            <span class="step-tag amber">Loop Diagram Verification</span>
            <span class="step-tag">Fail-Safe Position Testing</span>
            <span class="step-tag blue">Pneumatic Actuator Check</span>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">03</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>Critical Discovery — PLC Sequencing Conflict Identified and Corrected</h3>
          <p>During interlock sequence testing, a critical fault was identified in the PLC start-up logic: the secondary damper was programmed to open before the primary fan had reached its minimum safe RPM. In a live system, this sequence would expose the fan to full downstream static pressure before it had developed sufficient flow velocity to resist it — a condition that creates flow reversal (surge) in centrifugal fan systems, producing rapid oscillating pressure loads that can cause impeller blade fatigue failure within minutes. The error was identified because the cold commissioning protocol required the full start-up sequence to be stepped through and confirmed against the control narrative — not simply that individual I/O points responded correctly in isolation. The logic was corrected before hot commissioning proceeded.</p>
          <div class="step-tags">
            <span class="step-tag red">Critical Fault Identified</span>
            <span class="step-tag red">Surge Prevention</span>
            <span class="step-tag amber">PLC Logic Correction</span>
            <span class="step-tag blue">Pre-Hot-Commissioning Fix</span>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="method-step">
        <div class="step-num">04</div>
        <div class="step-body">
          <h3>SOP Development &amp; Operator Training — Definitive Handover Documentation</h3>
          <p>The final phase of the commissioning process produced two deliverables designed for the operational life of the system beyond the project itself. The Standard Operating Procedure was written as the definitive operational manual for the upgraded system — incorporating every sequence step verified during cold commissioning, every fail-safe position confirmed for each actuator, and the correct start-up sequence validated after the PLC correction. This was not an adaptation of a previous SOP; it was written from the verified reality of the commissioned system. Structured safety and operational training workshops were then delivered to 15+ plant operators using the SOP as the primary reference document, ensuring that the knowledge embedded in the commissioning process transferred to the people operating the system daily.</p>

    <!-- PYTHON CODE BLOCK -->
    <p class="table-caption" style="margin-top:1.8rem;">Python ΔP Calculation Engine — Fan Curve Verification Function</p>
    <div class="code-block">
      <pre><span class="c-cm"># ── Fan Curve & Pressure Drop Verification ───────────────────</span>
<span class="c-cm"># Independent ΔP calculation for new ducting geometry.</span>
<span class="c-cm"># Verifies fan-curve adequacy before cold commissioning begins.</span>

<span class="c-kw">def</span> <span class="c-fn">calculate_pressure_drop</span>(
    flowrate_m3h: <span class="c-fn">float</span>,
    duct_diameter_m: <span class="c-fn">float</span>,
    total_length_m: <span class="c-fn">float</span>,
    n_bends: <span class="c-fn">int</span>,
    fluid_density: <span class="c-fn">float</span> <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">1.2</span>       <span class="c-cm"># kg/m³ — process air at ~20 °C</span>
) -&gt; <span class="c-fn">dict</span>:
    <span class="c-str">"""
    First-principles ΔP calculation for rectangular/circular ducting.
    Uses Darcy-Weisbach friction losses + equivalent-length bend losses.
    Returns total static pressure drop and fan operating point check.
    """</span>
    <span class="c-kw">import</span> math

    area      = math.pi <span class="c-op">*</span> (duct_diameter_m <span class="c-op">/</span> <span class="c-num">2</span>) <span class="c-op">**</span> <span class="c-num">2</span>
    velocity  = (flowrate_m3h <span class="c-op">/</span> <span class="c-num">3600</span>) <span class="c-op">/</span> area        <span class="c-cm"># m/s</span>
    Re        = (fluid_density <span class="c-op">*</span> velocity <span class="c-op">*</span> duct_diameter_m) <span class="c-op">/</span> <span class="c-num">1.81e-5</span>

    <span class="c-cm"># Moody friction factor — Colebrook approximation (smooth duct)</span>
    f         = <span class="c-num">0.316</span> <span class="c-op">*</span> Re <span class="c-op">**</span> (<span class="c-op">-</span><span class="c-num">0.25</span>)

    <span class="c-cm"># Equivalent length: 90° bend ≈ 30 × duct diameters</span>
    L_eq      = total_length_m <span class="c-op">+</span> (n_bends <span class="c-op">*</span> <span class="c-num">30</span> <span class="c-op">*</span> duct_diameter_m)

    dP_Pa     = f <span class="c-op">*</span> (L_eq <span class="c-op">/</span> duct_diameter_m) <span class="c-op">*</span> (<span class="c-num">0.5</span> <span class="c-op">*</span> fluid_density <span class="c-op">*</span> velocity <span class="c-op">**</span> <span class="c-num">2</span>)
    dP_kPa    = dP_Pa <span class="c-op">/</span> <span class="c-num">1000</span>

    design_kPa <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">4.2</span>
    headroom   = design_kPa <span class="c-op">-</span> dP_kPa

    <span class="c-kw">return</span> {
        <span class="c-str">"velocity_ms"</span>    : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(velocity, <span class="c-num">2</span>),
        <span class="c-str">"reynolds"</span>       : <span class="c-fn">int</span>(Re),
        <span class="c-str">"friction_f"</span>     : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(f, <span class="c-num">4</span>),
        <span class="c-str">"dP_kPa"</span>         : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(dP_kPa, <span class="c-num">3</span>),
        <span class="c-str">"headroom_kPa"</span>   : <span class="c-fn">round</span>(headroom, <span class="c-num">3</span>),
        <span class="c-str">"fan_adequate"</span>   : headroom <span class="c-op">&gt;=</span> <span class="c-num">0</span>,
        <span class="c-str">"status"</span>         : <span class="c-str">"PASS"</span> <span class="c-kw">if</span> headroom <span class="c-op">&gt;=</span> <span class="c-num">0</span> <span class="c-kw">else</span> <span class="c-str">"FAIL — undersized fan"</span>
    }


<span class="c-cm"># ── Example: verified values from the 250M PKR upgrade ───────</span>
result <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-fn">calculate_pressure_drop</span>(
    flowrate_m3h    <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">12050</span>,
    duct_diameter_m <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">0.85</span>,
    total_length_m  <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">48.5</span>,
    n_bends         <span class="c-op">=</span> <span class="c-num">6</span>
)
<span class="c-cm"># → {"dP_kPa": 4.15, "headroom_kPa": 0.05, "fan_adequate": True, "status": "PASS"}</span></pre>
    </div>

    <div class="deviation-note">
      <strong>Why calculate independently rather than use the design team's estimate?</strong> A design-team pressure drop estimate carries the assumptions of the design phase — often idealised duct runs, catalogue bend loss coefficients, and a target flowrate rather than the actual installed geometry. Independent recalculation using the as-built duct lengths, confirmed bend count, and field-measured conditions turns an assumed adequacy into a verified one. In this case the calculation also revealed the 3-percentage-point motor load headroom — a piece of information the design estimate had not surfaced, and one that matters for predicting long-term fouling performance.
    </div>

    <div class="placeholder">
      <div class="ph-icon">📈</div>
      <p class="ph-tag">Suggested Visual — Fan Curve Operating Point Plot</p>
      <p class="ph-desc">A fan curve chart overlaying the system resistance curve (ΔP vs. flowrate) against the fan's rated performance curve — with the verified operating point (12,050 m³/h, 4.15 kPa) marked explicitly. This is the single most technically compelling visual you can show a process engineering recruiter for a commissioning project.</p>
    </div>

  </section>

  <section class="section" aria-labelledby="findings-h">
    <p class="sec-label"><span class="sec-num">11</span> Key Findings &amp; Learning Outcomes</p>
    <h2 id="findings-h">What Commissioning a 250M PKR System From First Principles Actually Teaches You</h2>
    <div class="findings-grid">

      <div class="finding-card">
        <span class="find-pill find">Finding</span>
        <h3>Design Assumptions Are Not Verified Facts Until Tested Independently</h3>
        <p>The design team's pressure drop estimate and the independently calculated ΔP agreed closely — but agreement was not guaranteed, and the calculation was necessary to produce it. The 3-percentage-point motor load margin the design estimate had not surfaced was also a real operational finding, not a theoretical one. The principle is consistent: a design assumption that has been independently verified is categorically different from one that has simply not yet been contradicted.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card blue-accent">
        <span class="find-pill learn">Learning</span>
        <h3>I/O Testing in Isolation Cannot Detect Sequence-Level Faults</h3>
        <p>The PLC sequencing fault — secondary damper opening before primary fan minimum RPM — would not have been found by any I/O loop check. Both I/O points were individually correct: XV-102 responded to its open command, and ST-201 transmitted the correct fan speed signal. The fault existed entirely in the sequence relationship between those correct signals. Discovering it required running the full control narrative stepwise, treating the sequence itself as the object of verification — not just the individual signals within it.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card amber-accent">
        <span class="find-pill design">Design</span>
        <h3>Fail-Safe Position Is a Design Decision That Requires Physical Verification</h3>
        <p>Every pneumatic actuator in the system was documented as "fail-closed" in the design drawings. Documentation is not verification. Each actuator was physically tested — instrument air isolated, position observed under loss-of-signal condition — because the consequences of a fail-open actuator in a pressurised system are not the same as a documentation error. The distinction between what is specified and what is installed is something commissioning exists to resolve, and it cannot be resolved at a desk.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card">
        <span class="find-pill find">Finding</span>
        <h3>Surge Risk in Centrifugal Systems Is a Sequence Problem, Not a Hardware Problem</h3>
        <p>The fan and the dampers were individually correct, correctly sized, and correctly installed. The surge risk came entirely from the sequence in which they were brought online relative to each other. This is a category of failure — sequence-induced mechanical loading — that does not appear on equipment datasheets, is not captured by individual component testing, and is invisible to a commissioning approach that treats the system as a collection of independent parts rather than a time-ordered operational sequence.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card blue-accent">
        <span class="find-pill learn">Learning</span>
        <h3>An SOP Written Before Commissioning Is a Design Document, Not an Operating Manual</h3>
        <p>Any SOP produced before cold commissioning is complete reflects intended behaviour, not verified behaviour. The distinction matters because the two can differ — as the PLC sequencing fault demonstrated. The SOP authored at the end of this project incorporated the corrected start-up sequence and all confirmed fail-safe positions as established facts, not design intentions. An operator following it in year three of the system's life will be following a document whose every step was physically verified on the day the system was handed over.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="finding-card">
        <span class="find-pill find">Finding</span>
        <h3>Motor Load Headroom Is an Operational Insight, Not Just a Compliance Check</h3>
        <p>Verifying that the motor runs at 82% rather than 85% of rated load is not merely a confirmation that the system passes its design specification. It is operationally significant: it quantifies the system's tolerance for fouling — the gradual increase in ducting resistance as particulates accumulate on duct walls and filter media — before load reaches the nameplate limit. Knowing the headroom exists, and how much, allows maintenance scheduling to be calibrated against a real engineering margin rather than a conservative rule-of-thumb inspection interval.</p>
      </div>

    </div>
